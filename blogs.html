How We Tune PID for Fast Tracks

We start by locking the robot to one repeatable test lane, then tune the proportional gain until the chassis begins to respond quickly without oscillating. From there, we add only enough integral correction to remove steady drift and keep the controller from “hunting” when the floor gets uneven.

The biggest mistake we see is tuning for a perfect room instead of the real one. Battery voltage drops, sensor reflectance changes, and cable flex all show up as noise. Our fix is to tune for the worst believable run, not the best one, and keep a simple log of every change so we can reverse a bad step fast.

PCB Review Checklist We Actually Use

Before sending a board out, we review the layout in three passes: power, signals, and assembly. Power nets get checked first, because a misplaced ground pour or undersized trace can ruin everything downstream. Once that passes, we look at routing symmetry, connector direction, and whether a human can actually plug the board in without guessing.

We also compare the schematic against the BOM and footprint library. Many of the problems we’ve hit were not electrical at all; they were footprint mismatches, swapped pin numbering, or a part that looked fine in the editor but was impossible to solder by hand. The checklist keeps those mistakes out before fabrication turns them into delays.

Firmware Architecture for Student Teams

We keep firmware predictable by splitting it into drivers, services, and application logic. Drivers only talk to hardware. Services translate that raw data into useful state. The app layer makes decisions. That separation means a new member can improve a sensor driver without accidentally changing how the robot decides to move.

The other rule is to keep interfaces boring. Small functions, clear names, and no hidden state make reviews easier and debugging much faster. If someone has to trace five files to understand one behavior, the architecture is already too tangled. Clean boundaries let a team grow without turning every edit into a risk.

January 2026

Debugging Sensor Noise in Real Labs

Lab noise usually comes from power rails, long jumper wires, and the fact that nothing stays still while you are testing. We start by reading the raw signal on its own, then compare it against known-good reference values. If the signal is noisy before code touches it, the problem is probably electrical, not algorithmic.

Filtering helps, but only after the wiring and sampling rate make sense. Oversampling a bad signal just gives you more bad data. We get better results by fixing grounding, shortening cables, and using a sampling window that matches the sensor’s real response time. That combination is usually enough to turn random spikes into something usable.

From Prototype to Demo Day in 10 Days

Our demo-day sprint begins by cutting the project down to one story: what should the audience see, in what order, and what can fail without breaking the presentation. Once that story is clear, we assign owners for hardware, firmware, and fallback prep so each part has one accountable person.

The last three days are reserved for integration and rehearsal. That is where most teams discover that their “almost done” parts do not actually fit together. We keep a spare power path, a simplified demo mode, and a printed checklist so the team can recover quickly if one subsystem misbehaves minutes before the show.

Common MCU Mistakes New Builders Make

New MCU projects often fail for boring reasons: wrong boot pins, weak grounding, or a board that never fully powers up. We teach members to verify power first, then confirm clock and reset behavior, and only after that move on to firmware upload. It saves a lot of time compared with chasing software before the chip is even alive.

Another common trap is assuming the board behaves like the datasheet example. Real wiring introduces resistance, noise, and human error. The habit that helps most is measuring at the board, not at the bench supply, and keeping a known-good reference sketch around. That gives everyone a stable baseline when a new design goes wrong.
